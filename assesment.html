Case Study

You’ve recently met Sandra, a 25-year-old single mother with a 6-year-old boy named Kevin. She graduated from college with a BA in Spanish about three years ago and has had many assorted short-term jobs from office administrative work to translation services, but at the end of every project she completes, she’s always told something along the lines of, “We’d love to bring you on permanently because we’re confident you would be a great employee, but our budget won’t allow it.”

Now Sandra is earning $13 an hour as a receptionist at a nonprofit. She was told when she started that she would only be working for three months to fill in for an employee on maternity leave. When Sandra is unemployed, she needs to use credit cards to meet basic expenses and her balances have ballooned to around $15,000, but she’s not totally sure because she hasn’t seen her credit report. Sandra also has $50,000 in federal student loan debt. She was able to get a deferment for a while, but now she’s getting bills saying that she owes $500/month. Kevin’s father left the house and went to Peru five years ago and Sandra has been unable to get him to contribute one penny in child support. Sandra sometimes even uses credit card cash advances to pay her rent. Her minimum balance payments are about $300 per month and the interest rate on her cards is about 19%. Each time Sandra needs to pay a bill, the rent, or go grocery shopping she has major difficulty getting enough money together to make the payment. She’s even considered going online to get a payday loan.
