Customer Maximizes Cash Flow

  • Roadblock: Customer Has A Monthly Cash Flow Deficit

    To close the deficit and move to a positive cash flow, explore a combination of strategies to help customers identify a plan that will work best for them.

    PLUG THE LEAKS

    Ask open-ended questions to help customers explore potential spending trade-offs. “How would you prioritize your expenses?” or “What’s most important to you?” Avoid judgmental questions that can lead the customer to become defensive, such as “Why do you spend so much at the salon?” or “Why don’t you quit smoking?” Help customers take a step back and look carefully at all their expenses to examine if their decisions are aligned with their financial goals.

    DEVELOP CREATIVE SOLUTIONS

    Explore cost-saving opportunities, such as buying a monthly transit card instead of a weekly one, buying certain groceries in bulk, discontinuing portions of a combined cable or phone package, and enrolling in special programs (see ACTION: CUSTOMER MAXIMIZES INCOME SUPPORTS). Customers can also take control of expenses by calling creditors and billing companies to change due dates to dates that work best for them.

    CLARIFY NECESSARY INCOME STREAM

    Helping customers decide on their “must-have” expenses allows them to recalculate their “must-have” income target. This calculation can provide a lot of clarity for customers as they review their expenses and take ownership of their spending plan.

    EXPLORE YEAR-ROUND TAX PLANNING

    Check out the TAXES MAP (see ACTION: CUSTOMER TAKES ADVANTAGE OF TAX INCENTIVES AND CREDITS).

    DISCUSS THE REFUND EARLY

    Explore ways of maximizing tax benefits. For some customers, the tax refund can be the largest source of income received all year. Start your discussion early about how refunds can close deficits and ultimately support financial goals. Refer to the 7 HEALTHY THINGS TO DO WITH YOUR REFUND WorkSheet.

    ADJUST WITHHOLDING ALLOWANCES

    Payroll departments use W-4 forms to calculate how much tax to withhold from each paycheck. They can be modified at any time, and for low-income families in particular, a change on this form can be a low-risk and high-impact strategy to improve cash flow. Online calculators (like paycheckcity.com/calculators.htm) can help map out different scenarios. If customers end up not paying enough taxes over the year through withholding, they may end up owing money at tax time. But never encourage customers to use inaccurate information on the W-4.

    EXAMINE DEBT LOAD

    Low-cost credit is a potential tool for an infrequent, short-term financial problem, but must be considered very carefully. Relying on credit is not a sustainable long-term strategy, since debts can quickly grow to the point that even the minimum balance payments become unaffordable. Review the DEBT MAP for debt management strategies.

Caution

If customers have done everything they can to pare down expenses, ask about other possibilities to increase income, such as investing in higher education or vocational training programs, exploring career path changes, or entrepreneurial opportunities. You can make a referral to a workforce development or small business development center. You can also revisit the ENVISIONING MY GOAL WorkSheet to ground the conversation. For some families, challenging financial times may open the door to relocating where they can access stronger support networks or better income-generating opportunities.

  • Customer Develops A Monthly Spending Plan

    Supporting customers to work on a meaningful spending plan can help them move toward proactive money management and away from a reactive approach where they may be largely “putting out fires.” Help customers understand that spending habits are not etched in stone, and that with a little bit of planning, they can maximize their bottom line. The first step is to get a concrete picture of how much money is going in and out of the household.

    WRITE IT DOWN

    The MONTHLY INCOME AND EXPENSES WorkSheet is a great place to start. For some expenses, customers may need to refer back to recent bills or start a daily or a weekly spending journal. The DAILY EXPENSES WorkSheet is an easy journaling tool.

    DO THE MATH

    Even the smallest expenses can add up. Tracking these expenses, even for a few days, can help customers make more informed spending decisions.

    DISCUSS DEBT LATER

    Debt servicing should not be addressed just yet. First, get the customer’s regular monthly expenses down on paper leaving out money to pay down debt. Understanding the customer’s basic expenses will lead to more productive discussions about effective debt-reduction strategies down the road.

Caution

If customers have no income, review ACTION: CUSTOMER MAXIMIZES INCOME SUPPORTS to investigate all possible supports such as food stamps, unemployment, and public assistance.

  • Customer Maximizes Income Supports

    As a financial coach, a clear understanding of income support programs and other money-saving opportunities tremendously impacts your ability to strategize with customers around all areas of financial planning. Check out some national benefits screening tools like helpengen.com and benefitscheckup.org.

    EXPLORE PUBLIC AND PRIVATE PROGRAMS

    Government-sponsored public benefits (Food Stamps, etc.) are vital programs, but are by no means the only option. Non governmental and privately-funded programs can range from discounted utility rates and hot meals to discounted phone service and summer youth programs. For example, see lifeline.gov, which provides information about discounted telephone service for low-income people, and dwss.nv.gov, which describes Nevada’s Energy Assistance Program. Some customer groups can often have multiple options (senior citizens, domestic violence survivors, families in need of “bridge” loans or grants for one-time emergencies).

    BUILD PARTNERSHIPS

    Develop relationships in the community and an understanding of eligibility requirements to strengthen direct referral networks whenever possible. Local partners that offer benefit screenings and facilitated enrollment for multiple programs at a single location can be especially helpful. For example, Single Stop USA (singlestop.org), which has sites in California, Florida, New Jersey, and New Mexico, provides low-income people with a broad range of services at a single location.

    COMPILE RESOURCES

    Research local and online resources and be prepared with handy print-outs that guide customers to specific programs and organizations. Programs increasingly offer online access to eligibility requirements, application guidelines, and “frequently asked questions."Some resources for Food Stamp and Social Security programs for example, are available here:

    Food Stamps: http://fns.usda.gov/snap/applicant_recipients/eligibility.htm

    SSI: http://ssa.gov/ssi/text-eligibility-ussi.htm

    SSDI and/or SSD: http://ssa.gov/dibplan/dqualify.htm

    Social Security Survivor Benefits: http://ssa.gov/pubs/10084.html

    MAP CHANGES IN CASH FLOW

    Work through the different scenarios with customers, helping them see the impact that enrolling would have on their savings and debt several months down the line.

    OFFER SUPPORT THROUGH THE ENROLLMENT PROCESS

    The enrollment process can often be long and sometimes frustrating. Serving as an accountability partner can be the final push needed to help customers’ secure critical benefits that can move them to the position to implement a steady savings strategy.

Caution

Help customers move past stigmas or concerns about public benefits. Sometimes customers are resistant to seek assistance. Probe further and determine the root of their reluctance. Remind customers that they are legally eligible for these benefits because they have paid into the system. For questions around eligibility requirements around immigration, tax and other enrollment implications, referrals to community partners and experts in the respective fields might be your best option. Also, check out HO310's Assessment Tab on Money Scripts

  • Customer Maximizes Employee Benefits

    Many employers offer valuable benefits, but employees often don’t know what’s available, how to enroll, and how the benefit programs can have a positive impact on customers’ financial security and help them reach their goals.

    UNDERSTAND THE PACKAGE

    An employee benefits package can be overwhelming. Help customers to take the first step by having them bring in their package to review together. Ask about everything from matched savings and legal services, to scholarships and tuition reimbursement for employees and their families. Encourage customers to connect with local union representatives and/or Human Resources Department to get a full explanation of all benefit possibilities.

    REVIEW THE BENEFITS OF PRE-TAX PROGRAMS

    Participating in employee programs may not only help customers save for retirement, but often can result in significant tax savings in the immediate tax year. Because of the tax advantages, well-planned contributions may have minimal impact on customers’ take-home pay. Be sure to check out the PRE-TAX DOLLARS: MONEY BACK IN YOUR POCKET TipSheet and incorporate transportation benefits, flexible health spending plans, and employer-matched 401(k)s and 403(b)s into the MONTHLY INCOME AND EXPENSES WorkSheet.

    CALCULATE THE "FREE MONEY" THAT MAY BE LEFT BEHIND

    Help customers understand employer-matched retirement programs. Do the math together and show customers the amount of money they may be leaving money on the table. Even if 401(k) and 403(b) contributions are not matched by an employer, customers who are not refunded all of their income taxes at the end of the year should consider the potentially “effortless” impact of making even small monthly contributions.

Caution

Some customers may view their retirement plan as an emergency savings account. Unless they are in an extremely unique situation, customers should not consider withdrawing money from their retirement accounts as a last resort to pay down debts. Review the penalties for early withdrawal and the missed opportunities for compounding so customers understand the dangers of tapping into their retirement savings. You can also ease their concerns by explaining that retirement plans are protected from debt collectors

  • Customer Puts Child Care Plan In Place

    Child care can be a tremendous factor in a family’s financial planning, especially for single-parent households. Customers should explore creative options to reduce costs, keeping in mind that quality child care is of paramount importance.

    KNOW YOUR LOCAL AND STATE PROGRAMS

    In addition to considering relatives and other support networks, low-cost day care centers and after-school programs might also be available. Customers receiving public assistance or exiting such programs may be eligible for child care benefits, and in certain states, four-year-olds are also able to attend Universal Pre-K programs. For example, Care 4 Kids (ctcare4kids.com) is a resource to assist certain low-income Connecticut families obtain funding to make quality child care affordable.

    CALCULATE THE COST OF DIFFERENT OPTIONS

    Help customers plan ahead and map out the financial impact of each child care option. The cost of transportation, extra fees for the occasions customers have to work late, and even the effect on customers’ psychological and emotional well-being, are all extremely important factors to consider and integrate into the design of a viable monthly spending plan. The MONTHLY INCOME AND EXPENSES WorkSheet can help drill down on these costs.
